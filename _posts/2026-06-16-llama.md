# LLaMA



## 1. Problem

By 2022, large language models were rapidly improving through scaling. Models such as GPT-3, PaLM, and Gopher demonstrated that increasing model size could lead to strong performance across a wide range of NLP tasks.

However, reproducing these models was practically impossible for most researchers. The strongest models relied on proprietary datasets, undocumented data sources, and enormous computational resources. As a result, the research community could study the results of these models, but could not fully reproduce or extend them.

At the same time, it was becoming increasingly unclear whether simply increasing parameter counts was the most efficient path forward. GPT-3 used 175B parameters, yet recent research suggested that many large language models were undertrained relative to their size.

---

## 2. Motivation

The authors were motivated by two observations.

First, the Chinchilla scaling law (Hoffmann et al., 2022) challenged the prevailing belief that larger models are always better.

> Chinchilla showed that, for a fixed compute budget, smaller models trained on significantly more tokens can outperform much larger models trained on less data.

This suggested that model performance depends not only on parameter count, but also on the balance between model size and training data. In other words, scaling data can be just as important as scaling parameters.

Second, the most capable language models were becoming increasingly closed. Many relied on proprietary datasets that were unavailable to the broader research community, making scientific progress difficult to verify and reproduce.

LLaMA was designed to test whether a state-of-the-art language model could be built using only publicly available data while remaining computationally efficient at inference time. The goal was not simply to build a larger model, but to build a model that achieved the best possible performance for a given inference budget.
